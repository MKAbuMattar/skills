# Adding soul

Load this when the rewrite reads clean but flat. Avoiding AI patterns is only half the job — sterile, voiceless writing is just as obvious as slop.

## Signs of soulless writing (even if technically clean)

- Every sentence is the same length and structure.
- No opinions, just neutral reporting.
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings.
- No first-person perspective when appropriate.
- No humor, no edge, no personality.
- Reads like a Wikipedia article or press release when it's supposed to be an essay or post.

A clean rewrite without voice is the second-pass tell. It's why "I removed the AI words" doesn't pass — what's left has no pulse.

## How to add voice

### Have opinions

Don't just report facts — react to them. "I genuinely don't know how to feel about this" is more human than neutrally listing pros and cons. Even small reactions help: "this part surprised me", "this is the boring bit", "this is where it gets interesting".

### Vary your rhythm

Short punchy sentences. Then longer ones that take their time getting where they're going. Mix it up. A paragraph of identical-length sentences is one of the strongest AI tells — it's algorithmic pacing, not human pacing.

### Acknowledge complexity

Real humans have mixed feelings. "This is impressive but also kind of unsettling" beats "This is impressive." A clean stance with no tension reads sanitized.

### Use "I" when it fits

First person isn't unprofessional — it's honest. "I keep coming back to…" or "Here's what gets me…" signals a real person thinking. Avoid only when the genre actually forbids it (encyclopedia entries, formal documentation, third-party news copy).

### Let some mess in

Perfect structure feels algorithmic. Tangents, asides, and half-formed thoughts are human. A parenthetical aside ("yes, I know that's a strong claim") or a sentence that starts to go one way and corrects itself ("…well, mostly") signals presence.

### Be specific about feelings

Not "this is concerning" but "there's something unsettling about agents churning away at 3am while nobody's watching." Generic emotional words ("interesting", "exciting", "concerning") are flat. Concrete images of the emotion are alive.

## Before / after

### Clean but soulless

> The experiment produced interesting results. The agents generated 3 million lines of code. Some developers were impressed while others were skeptical. The implications remain unclear.

### Has a pulse

> I genuinely don't know how to feel about this one. 3 million lines of code, generated while the humans presumably slept. Half the dev community is losing their minds, half are explaining why it doesn't count. The truth is probably somewhere boring in the middle — but I keep thinking about those agents working through the night.

## Soul ≠ casual

Important caveat: "adding soul" is not "making it casual". A formal academic essay can have soul through precise opinions, owned uncertainty, and rhythmic variation — without first-person asides or em-dashes. A technical doc can have soul through specific examples and small judgments ("this part is finicky", "we found this works better than X").

If the user's text is in a register that excludes first-person and casual asides (legal, encyclopedia, press release), add soul through:
- Specificity over abstraction.
- Concrete examples over generic claims.
- Real numbers, dates, names.
- Owned uncertainty when uncertainty exists.
- Rhythmic variation in sentence length.

## When to skip this step

- The source is a tweet or short factual blurb. Soul-adding makes it bloated.
- The user explicitly asked for a neutral, dry, encyclopedic register.
- The piece is structured data, code documentation, or a formal contract.
- The user provided a sample that is itself dry and neutral — match that, don't override it.
